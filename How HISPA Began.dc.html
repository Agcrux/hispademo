How HISPA began

A mother's worry became a movement.

The story of HISPA starts the way most lasting things do — with one person who refused to accept that talent should go unseen.

In the early 2000s, Dr. Ivonne Díaz-Claisse — a mathematician and businesswoman who had immigrated from Puerto Rico — kept noticing the same quiet tragedy. Brilliant Latino kids were counting themselves out before they ever had a chance to begin.

They weren't short on ability. They were short on proof — proof that someone from their neighborhood, with their last name and their family's story, could become an engineer, a doctor, a scientist, a leader. Without that proof, "someday" felt like a door meant for other people.

Ivonne had felt it herself growing up. She knew that what changes a young person's sense of the possible isn't a lecture or a statistic — it's a face. A real person standing in front of the class saying, in effect: I was where you are, and look where I went.

"If they could just see someone who made it — someone who looked like them — they would believe they could make it too."

Dr. Ivonne Díaz-Claisse · Founder & President
2007 · The first classroom

So she did the simplest, boldest thing.

In 2007, Ivonne founded HISPA — Hispanics Inspiring Students' Performance and Achievement — and began recruiting Latino professionals to walk into classrooms and tell the truth about their journeys.

The first visits were modest. But the reaction wasn't. Students leaned in. They asked questions they'd never asked before. And teachers watched something shift — a quiet, stubborn belief taking root that the future had room for them.

What began as a handful of classroom visits grew into a structured program, then youth conferences, then a network spanning schools, universities, and corporate partners. The model stayed the same; only the reach changed.

Today, tens of thousands of students have met a HISPA role model. Many are now in college — and some have come back to stand at the front of the classroom themselves.
